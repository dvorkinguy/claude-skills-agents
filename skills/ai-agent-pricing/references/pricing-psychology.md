# Pricing Psychology

## Core Principles

### 1. Anchor High

Always show the most expensive option first. It makes everything else feel reasonable.

**Wrong:**
| Starter $297 | Growth $697 | Scale $1,997 |

**Right:**
| Scale $1,997 | Growth $697 | Starter $297 |

Or better - show Scale as the default/recommended option.

### 2. The Decoy Effect

Add an option that makes your target option look better.

**Without decoy:**
- Basic: $297/mo (1 agent)
- Pro: $697/mo (5 agents)

**With decoy:**
- Basic: $297/mo (1 agent)
- Plus: $597/mo (2 agents) ← Decoy
- Pro: $697/mo (5 agents) ← Target

The Plus plan makes Pro look like a bargain (2.5× more value for $100).

### 3. Charm Pricing

Prices ending in 7 or 9 perform better:
- $297 > $300 (feels researched, specific)
- $997 > $1,000 (stays in "hundreds" mentally)
- $2,997 > $3,000 (psychological barrier)

But avoid: $299.99 (feels cheap, discount store)

### 4. Price the Pain, Not the Product

**Wrong:** "AI agent subscription: $697/mo"

**Right:** "Eliminate 40 hours of data entry weekly: $697/mo"

Always connect price to the problem solved, not the thing delivered.

### 5. Show What They Lose, Not What They Get

Loss aversion is 2× stronger than gain motivation.

**Weak:** "Save 40 hours per week"
**Strong:** "Stop losing 40 hours every week"

**Weak:** "Reduce errors by 90%"
**Strong:** "Eliminate 90% of costly mistakes"

## Presentation Strategies

### The Contrast Frame

Show the alternative costs first:

> "Hiring a full-time employee: $6,000/mo + benefits + training + management overhead = $10,000/mo
>
> Our AI agent: $697/mo. Same output. No management. No sick days."

### The Daily Cost

Break monthly into daily to minimize perceived cost:

> "$697/mo = $23/day. That's less than lunch. And it saves you 2 hours daily."

### The Opportunity Cost

Show what they're losing by not acting:

> "Every week you wait costs you $2,000 in wasted labor. That's $8,000/month, $96,000/year. Our solution is $8,364/year. The math is simple."

### The Investment Frame

Never say "cost" or "price" - say "investment":

- "The investment is $697/month"
- "Your investment includes..."
- "For this investment, you receive..."

## Visual Psychology

### The Recommended Badge

One option should always be marked as recommended/popular:

```
| Basic | **Pro** ★ Popular | Enterprise |
         ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
         This gets 60% of selections
```

### Highlight Savings

Show the calculation visibly:

```
Annual Plan
$697/mo × 12 = $8,364
~~$8,364~~ $6,691/year
You save: $1,673 (20%)
```

### Use Visual Hierarchy

Make the option you want them to pick:
- Larger
- More colorful
- Centered
- With more features listed

### Feature Checkmarks

Green checkmarks ✓ feel inclusive
Red X marks ✗ feel exclusionary (use sparingly)

## Pricing Page Structure

### Above the Fold
- Clear headline: What problem you solve
- Price anchoring: Show value first
- 3-4 tier options maximum

### Social Proof
- Logos of clients
- "Join 500+ companies"
- Testimonials with results

### Risk Reversal
- Money-back guarantee
- Free trial
- "Cancel anytime"

### Call to Action
- One clear button per tier
- Action words: "Start", "Get", "Begin"
- Not passive: "Learn more", "See details"

## Negotiation Psychology

### The Door-in-the-Face

Start with a high ask, then "compromise" to your real target:

> You: "The complete package is $3,997/month."
> Them: "That's too much."
> You: "I understand. What if we started with the core automation at $1,997?"

The $1,997 feels like a win for them.

### The Foot-in-the-Door

Start with a small commitment, then expand:

> "Let's start with a pilot at $297/month. Once you see the results, we can discuss expanding to the full solution."

### Time Scarcity

Limited-time offers work. But only if believable:

**Weak:** "50% off this week only!" (feels fake)

**Strong:** "Locking in 2024 pricing before our January increase" (feels real)

### Social Proof

> "Most of our clients at your size go with the Growth plan"

> "Companies like [similar company] typically see ROI in 30 days"

## Red Flags to Avoid

### Don't Compete on Price
- Race to the bottom = death
- Someone will always be cheaper
- Compete on value, speed, expertise

### Don't Over-Discount
- More than 20% = desperation
- Conditions required for any discount
- Never discount without getting something

### Don't Apologize for Price
- "I know it's expensive, but..." = weak
- State price confidently, then stop talking

### Don't Offer Too Many Options
- 3-4 options maximum
- Too many = paralysis
- Make the choice easy

## Testing Your Pricing

### A/B Test These:
- Price points ($297 vs $347)
- Tier names (Pro vs Growth vs Business)
- Feature bundling
- Annual discount percentage

### Metrics to Track:
- Conversion rate per tier
- Average revenue per user
- Churn by price point
- Time to decision

### Raise Prices When:
- Conversion rate >20% (you're too cheap)
- No price objections (you're too cheap)
- You can't handle the volume (you're too cheap)
